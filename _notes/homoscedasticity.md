These notes began as a favor. A friend — a quantitative analyst at a bank —
shared a set of model-validation interview questions he thought would be useful
practice. He is referred to throughout this series as the Banker.

One of those questions concerned homoscedasticity and how to test for it. That
single question did not stay small: working through it properly required
conditional expectation, the incidental-parameters problem, pivotal quantities,
likelihood theory, and eventually the Breusch–Pagan test in full — enough
material to fill twelve parts.

The mathematical development throughout was carried out jointly with a
colleague specializing in probability theory, who consulted on the framework,
caught errors, and proposed several of the key formulations. He is referred to
throughout the text as the Probabilist, and appears as a running dialogue
partner rather than a silent contributor — most sections are structured as an
exchange between the two of us.

Both names are descriptive epithets, not identifiers. They stand in for two
people who preferred not to be named in a document that may eventually be public.

This series exists mainly because working through a problem in writing — arguing
it out, catching my own mistakes, redoing the parts that didn't hold up — is how
I actually learn. What follows is not a polished treatise; it is the record of
that process, shared as it stands.
